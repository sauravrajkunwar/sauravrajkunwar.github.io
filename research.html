Job Market Paper

Pricing Agricultural Environmental Services Under Policy-Consistent Normalizations

Conservation programs differ in how they reward farmers: practice-based programs compensate for adopting management practices, while outcome-based programs pay for measured environmental improvements. Yet the private costs of compliance under these alternative designs remain unclear.

We develop a duality-based framework to value agricultural environmental services under policy-consistent normalizations. A normalization selects a numeraire that reflects program objectives and determines how trade-offs among market and environmental outcomes are evaluated.

Using Illinois corn production data, we show that practice-based normalizations result in lower shadow prices for carbon sequestration and greenhouse gas abatement, while outcome-based normalizations produce higher costs. Erosion control shows limited variation.

By establishing shadow prices as policy-dependent measures, the framework provides a consistent basis for comparing conservation programs and setting incentive payments that align private management with environmental goals.

Project 1

What are the carbon services from cover-crop adoption worth from farmers' perspective?

We derive shadow prices of carbon services provided by cover crops relative to non-cover-crop agricultural practices, accounting for carbon sequestration and greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions.

We model agricultural technology by integrating crop production, carbon sequestration, and GHG emissions. Using a data envelopment analysis (DEA), we derive a novel functional representation of the system.

We then calculate shadow prices through the dual representation of the technology and compare shadow prices between cover-crop and non-cover-crop fields. We estimate that carbon sequestration and GHG emission reduction services from cover-crop fields are valued at USD 17.88 and USD 9.42 per acre, respectively.

We compare these values to payments from incentive programs promoting cover-crop adoption, highlighting economic feasibility and barriers to sustainable practice adoption.

Project 2

Agricultural Productivity Index Decomposition with Weather and Adaptation Indicators

Extreme weather events such as excessive heat, droughts, and intense rainfall are becoming more frequent. These conditions create risks for consistent crop yields and quality.

Sustainable agricultural practices are gaining importance as farmers adapt to climatic variability. Cover cropping and conservation tillage are widely recognized approaches that can help mitigate the impacts of extreme weather.

This study evaluates the role of adaptation measures in agricultural production. We investigate whether environmentally friendly management practices improve agricultural productivity and how these practices contribute to climate resilience.
