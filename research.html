Pricing Agricultural Environmental Services Under Policy-Consistent Normalizations

Conservation programs differ in how they reward farmers: practice-based programs compensate for adopting management practices, while outcome-based programs pay for measured environmental improvements. Yet the private costs of compliance under these alternative designs remain unclear. We develop a duality-based framework to value agricultural environmental services under policy-consistent normalizations. The normalization defines how trade-offs among market and environmental outcomes are evaluated. Using Illinois corn production data, we show that practice-based normalizations result in lower shadow prices for carbon sequestration and greenhouse gas abatement, while outcome-based normalizations yield higher values. Erosion control shows limited variation. This framework provides a consistent basis for comparing conservation programs and designing incentive payments aligned with environmental goals.

Project 1

Carbon Services from Cover-Crop Adoption

We derive shadow prices of carbon services provided by cover crops relative to non-cover-crop practices, incorporating sequestration and greenhouse gas reductions.

Using a data envelopment analysis (DEA) representation, we compute shadow prices based on the dual representation of agricultural technology and compare values across cover-crop and non-cover-crop fields.

We find that carbon sequestration and GHG emission reduction from cover-crop fields are worth USD 17.88 and USD 9.42 per acre, respectively. We compare these values to payments from incentive programs promoting sustainable adoption.

Project 2

Agricultural Productivity Index Decomposition with Weather Indicators

Extreme weather events — including heat waves, droughts, and intense precipitation — are becoming increasingly common due to climate change, posing challenges for maintaining stable crop yields.

As climatic variability increases, sustainable management practices such as cover cropping and conservation tillage gain importance as buffers against weather-related risks.

This project evaluates how adaptation measures can mitigate weather impacts on productivity, and whether environmentally friendly practices enhance long-term agricultural resilience.
